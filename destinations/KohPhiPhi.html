Koh Phi Phi Travel Guide

Andaman Sea · Dramatic limestone islands, Maya Bay and lively Tonsai

Koh Phi Phi is one of Thailand’s most famous island groups, known for towering limestone cliffs, turquoise water and the iconic Maya Bay. The main inhabited island is Phi Phi Don, with the village of Tonsai as the busy centre. Phi Phi Leh, home to Maya Bay, is uninhabited and visited by boat. The scenery is genuinely spectacular, but the islands are also very popular, especially in high season. Expect crowds in the main areas, lively nightlife in Tonsai, and some of the most photographed views in the country.

How to Get There

There is no airport on Phi Phi. The only way in is by boat from Phuket or Krabi.

From Phuket
Ferries and speedboats leave from Rassada Pier. Ferries take about 2 hours; speedboats take around 45–60 minutes. Combined transfer-and-boat tickets are easy to arrange from the airport or hotels.

From Krabi / Ao Nang
Boats leave from Ao Nang or nearby piers. Journey time is roughly 45–90 minutes depending on the boat type. This is often the slightly shorter and cheaper option.

All visitors pay a small island entry/cleaning fee (around 20 baht) on arrival. Once on Phi Phi Don, the main village is walkable. Longtail boats are used to reach other beaches and for island tours.

Climate & Best Time to Visit

Phi Phi follows the Andaman coast pattern.

Best time: November to April
Dry weather, calm seas and the best visibility for snorkelling and boat trips. December to February is peak season with the highest prices and largest crowds.

Shoulder months: May and October
Still usable, with fewer people and lower rates, though rain becomes more frequent.

Rainy season: June to September
Heavier rain and rougher seas. Some boat tours are cancelled or limited. The islands are quieter and greener, but the main outdoor appeal is reduced.

What You’ll Love Doing Here

Maya Bay on Phi Phi Leh is the big draw. Made famous by the film *The Beach*, it is now carefully managed. Swimming is restricted to protect the coral, but walking the beach and seeing the cliffs remains impressive. Most visitors come on a morning boat tour that also includes nearby lagoons and snorkelling stops.

On Phi Phi Don, the two main beaches at the centre are Tonsai Bay (where the boats arrive) and Loh Dalum on the opposite side. The village between them is packed with restaurants, bars, shops and nightlife. It is lively and can feel intense.

The short but steep hike to the Phi Phi Viewpoint is one of the best things you can do. From the top you get the famous panoramic view of the twin bays and the narrow strip of land that connects them. Go early or late to avoid the worst heat and crowds.

Other options include Long Beach (a short boat ride away), Monkey Beach, snorkelling trips, and simply exploring the smaller bays by longtail. Nightlife in Tonsai is energetic and continues late — great if that suits you, less so if you want quiet.

Pros and Cons

What people love

  • Spectacular limestone scenery and turquoise water
  • Iconic Maya Bay and the viewpoint over the twin bays
  • Easy day trips and boat tours from the main village
  • Lively atmosphere and good range of food and bars
  • Convenient access from both Phuket and Krabi

What can frustrate people

  • Tonsai can feel overcrowded and noisy, especially at night
  • High season brings heavy boat traffic and higher prices
  • Some beaches and reefs show the effects of heavy tourism
  • Limited space and steep paths — not ideal for those with mobility issues
  • Rainy season restricts boat activities significantly

Why Go

Koh Phi Phi delivers some of the most dramatic island scenery in Thailand. Come for Maya Bay, the viewpoint, the longtail-boat rides between limestone cliffs, and a few days of classic Andaman island life. Stay in or near Tonsai if you want the energy and convenience; look at Long Beach or the quieter ends of the island if you prefer a calmer base. It is busy and touristy, but the landscape is still powerful enough to make the trip worthwhile for most visitors.
